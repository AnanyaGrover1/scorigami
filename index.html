What is Scorigami?

Inspired by Jon Bois’s original project on the history of the NFL, Scorigami charts every unique score in Princeton football during the modern era. For the purpose of this project, the “modern era” begins in 1912, when the touchdown was changed from 5 to 6 points, as it is today. Due to the nature of scoring in football — in which points are most regularly accrued in chunks of two, three, six, and seven — there are many final scores which have never happened. By the end of the 2022 season, Princeton football played 961 games since 1912, 455 (47 percent) of which were score Scorigamis.

For example, in the 2022 season, Princeton beat Lafayette 23-2. Princeton had never played in a game that ended 23-2 – it was a Scorigami! These unique scores are actually quite common when playing Lafayette; in the 31 games Princeton has played them, 18 resulted in scores that had never happened before in Princeton’s history. Other common opponents with over half of the games resulting in Scorigamis include Brown, Colgate, and Rutgers. 2022’s 37-10 win over Harvard marked the continuation of a five-year Scorigami streak between the two rivals (all of which Princeton won).

Why is this data meaningful?

Princeton played in the first game in the history of college football on November 6, 1869. Since then, they’ve played over 1,300 games against 80 different opponents, winning 28 national championships and a Heisman trophy. As Jerry Price, the historian for Princeton Athletics, puts it, “Princeton football is a real source of pride. Its history is very storied.”

Since Bob Surace ’90 became Princeton’s head football coach in 2010, he has seen 61 Scorigamis in his 120 games – after his twelfth season, he has the second highest scorigami/season average of all coaches that coached for more than two seasons at 5.08.

Surace knows how important history is to the team and alumni. “When I meet [a football alum], and it’s very humbling. I knew the passion of our alumni, and some research they've done. The history goes way back, and I’m quite humbled.”

Where is the data from?

The data is pulled and verified using multiple sources: Athletics at Princeton: A History by Frank Presbrey, Bric-a-Bracs, and The Daily Princetonian’s archives.
